Python Developer in the SAIL Lab at USC: 2018 (summer)

This summer I had the pleasure of working in the SAIL (Signal Analysis and Interpretation Lab) at USC (University of Souther California) as an undergraduate researcher. I worked with Tanner Sorensen to create spatio-temporal atlases of real-time MRI images of speech production and then use said atlases in order to automatically segment the relevant curves that impact speech production (upper lip, lower lip, tongue/epiglottis, mandible, chin, hard palate, velum, etc.). We created this process to be used as a precursor to a more precise machine learning algorithmic process to segment the curves. To create this process, we built spatio-temporal atlases (base photo of a participant that spans multiple frames) for 4 participants to start out. We then created multiple types of spatio-temporal atlases in order to gauge which would be most effective as a starting point for the segmentation process. We created 4 types of atlases for each participant. Once we had the atlases, we used the Elastix and ITK medical imagine libraries in order to transform every other frame of the rtMRI photos of each participant saying the same sentence against each of the atlases. We then selected 10 images that encompassed the full range of human speech production in order to test the accuracy of our segmentation. After manually segmenting the 10 images, we then took the manual segmentations of the relevant atlas and transformed those points based on the specific transform generated for each image. I then created a script in order to evaluate the segmentations across 10 transforms which ended up being around 300 unique photos in order to calculate the P2CP (point to closest point in pixels) distance between the manual segmentations and the automatic segmentations. For our ideal transform methodology, our average P2CP error was around 0.9-1. Given that the voxel size of the images was 2.84, these results were incredibly impressive for this unique segmentation process. After gathering our results, Tanner and I wrote a paper detailing our process, our results, and the future direction for this segmentation process and what can be accomplished with said process. This paper should be published in the coming months.

Developing Cryptocurrency Trading Tools: 2017-2018

One thing I am incredibly passionate about is cryptocurrency and how blockchain technology will change the world we live in. I was contacted by someone after detailing some of my solo cryptocurrency projects in the works on Reddit. He ended up paying me to create some trading tools using Binance to give him a market advantage over other traders on Binance. I ended up coding an automated trading client and arbitrage bot in Python from scratch. The Trading Client automates a reliable trading process, enabling the user to automate buys, sells, and stop losses in order to gain a market advantage over every other user on Binance (aside from those who are also using automated traders). On the other side, the arbitrage bot abuses price discrepancies between two cryptocurrency exchanges (Binance and Kucoin) for a range of cryptocurrencies (BTC, ETH, LTC, ICX, VET, NANO, etc.). It seeks out a price discrepancy of up to 3% or more (given that the fees are generally around 1-2%) and buys on one exchange and sells on the other. The software then texts the user to rebalance the assets in order to reset the ability to arbitrage, given that this arbitrage bot utilizes transferless arbitrage. I am now currently working on automating the price decisions for buys and sells in order to fully automate the trading process without any human interaction. I have currently written the RSI calculator (accurate within 0.01 of Binance's calculations) and am currently working on Bollinger Bands and MACD as verifiers in order to make decisions on whether to buy or sell.

Brown University Microbiology: Summer 2015

In the summer of 2015, I worked with Dr. William Fairbrother on research regarding the disruption of pre-mRNA splicing, a common mechanic for many disease mutations (>30% of all hereditary dieases mutations may be due to disruptions in RNA splicing). I used Real-Time PCR analysis and cell culturing methods to create these disruptions (single nucleotide polymorphisms) and then analyze and catalog the specific splices in order to determine the effects on cultured cells.
